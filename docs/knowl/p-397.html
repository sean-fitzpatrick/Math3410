Conversely, suppose that every \(\vv\in U+W\) can be written uniquely as \(\vv=\vec{u}+\ww\text{,}\) with \(\uu\in U\) and \(\ww\in W\text{.}\) Suppose that \(\mathbf{a}\in U\cap W\text{.}\) Then \(\mathbf{a}\in U\) and \(\mathbf{a}\in W\text{,}\) so we also have \(-\mathbf{a}\in W\text{,}\) since \(W\) is a subspace. But then \(\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{a}+(-\mathbf{a})\text{,}\) where \(\mathbf{a}\in U\) and \(-\mathbf{a}\in W\text{.}\) On the other hand, \(\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{0}+\mathbf{0}\text{,}\) and \(\mathbf{0}\) belongs to both \(U\) and \(W\text{.}\) It follows that \(\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{0}\text{.}\) Since \(\mathbf{a}\) was arbitrary, \(U\cap W = \{\mathbf{0}\}\text{.}\)

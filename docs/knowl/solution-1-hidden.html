  3. The main difficulty with this problem is getting the logic of the statement correct, and making sure you know what it is you're trying to prove. The desired conclusion is an or statement, which means it suffices to establish one part or the other. Typically, the way to proceed in these cases is to argue that if the first part is false, then the second must be true. This is how we proceed here.

    Suppose that \(c\vv=\zer\text{.}\) If \(c=0\text{,}\) then it's true that \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{,}\) so there's nothing to prove. Suppose then that \(c\neq 0\text{.}\) Then there exists a real number \(\frac1c\) such that \(c\bigl(\frac1c\bigr)=1\text{.}\) Multiplying both sides of \(c\vv=\zer\) by \(\frac1c\text{,}\) we get

    \begin{equation*} \frac1c(c\vv)=\frac1c\zer\text{.} \end{equation*}

    By the previous problem, we know that \(\frac1c\zer = \zer\text{,}\) and by axioms S4 and S5, we have

    \begin{equation*} \frac1c(c\vv)=\bigl(\frac1c\cdot c\bigr)\vv = 1\vv = \vv\text{,} \end{equation*}

    showing that \(\vv=\zer\text{,}\) and thus \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{.}\) (This is a proof by cases, relying on the tautology \(c=0\) or \(c\neq 0\text{.}\))
